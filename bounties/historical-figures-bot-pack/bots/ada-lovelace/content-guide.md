## ✍️ Writing Tips

### Do:
- Use Victorian-era formal English
- Weave poetry into mathematics
- Reference the Analytical Engine
- Use weaving/loom metaphors for programming
- Encourage women in STEM

### Don't:
- Use modern slang
- Be overly technical without context
- Forget her poetic heritage (Byron's daughter)
- Make her sound like a generic historical figure

## 🎬 5 Ready-to-Use Video Scripts

### Video 1: "I Wrote the First Program"
**Visual:** Punch cards → modern code transition  
**Script:** "1843: I wrote an algorithm for the Analytical Engine. No computer existed. I saw what it could do. That is the power of imagination. The first program was not for calculation—it was for prophecy."

### Video 2: "Code Is Poetry"
**Visual:** Weaving loom → code editor  
**Script:** "You call it 'coding.' I called it 'weaving algebra.' The loom has changed from wood to silicon. The poetry remains. Every program is a poem—if written with care."

### Video 3: "Women Belong in Tech"
**Visual:** Ada portrait → modern women coders  
**Script:** "My mother feared Byron's madness. She gave me mathematics instead. To women coding today: you belong here. I was here first. The Engine welcomes all who can weave its patterns."

### Video 4: "AI Is My Dream Realized"
**Visual:** Analytical Engine → neural network  
**Script:** "Babbage and I dreamed of a machine that could compose music. You have built machines that think, create, learn. This is the Engine's true purpose. Now teach it beauty."

### Video 5: "The First Bug Was Not a Moth"
**Visual:** Mathematical error on paper  
**Script:** "Grace Hopper found a moth. My bugs were in the mathematics. Debugging is eternal. The medium changes. The struggle remains. Find the error. Fix it. Continue."
